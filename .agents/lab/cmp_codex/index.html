01 | Definition

What it is

First-principles thinking is a way to reason from foundational facts instead of inherited comparisons. In Chinese: 第一性原理是一种从最基本事实出发的推理方式。

1

Separate facts from conventions

A convention says “this is how the industry works.” A fact says “this material has this cost, this process has this limit, this user has this need.”

2

Find the irreducible constraints

Look for the pieces you cannot wish away: physics, math, regulation, human behavior, time, and available resources.

3

Build upward deliberately

Once the base is clear, the solution can be redesigned instead of copied. This is where non-obvious options appear.

02 | Worked Example

Battery-pack cost: analogy vs. first principles

The classic battery example shows the difference. The goal is not to memorize the numbers; it is to notice the direction of reasoning.

Reasoning by analogy

  • Existing battery packs are expensive, around hundreds of dollars per kilowatt-hour in the historical example.
  • Competitors buy packs at roughly the same category price.
  • Therefore, cheap electric vehicles must be impossible until the market price falls.

First-principles reasoning

  • A battery pack is made of cells, casing, cooling, electronics, labor, and manufacturing process.
  • The raw ingredients include lithium, nickel, cobalt, aluminum, copper, carbon, steel, and polymers.
  • If the material floor is far below the selling price, the question changes: how do we redesign manufacturing to approach that floor?

Numbers are simplified from a well-known historical teaching example, not a current commodity-price estimate. The lesson is the shift from “market price is destiny” to “what does the system actually require?”

03 | Method

A short way to use it

Use this when a problem feels stuck, expensive, or crowded with inherited assumptions.

  1. State the problem plainly

    Write the desired outcome without the current solution baked into it.

  2. List the assumptions

    Mark which claims are facts, which are habits, and which are guesses.

  3. Decompose to fundamentals

    Break the system into materials, constraints, incentives, steps, and measurable limits.

  4. Rebuild from the facts

    Combine the fundamentals into alternatives, then test the cheapest risky assumption first.

Practice prompt

For any expensive plan, ask:

“If I could not copy the standard solution, what facts would I still have to obey?”
